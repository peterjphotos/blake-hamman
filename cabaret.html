Critical Response

A Review by
Byrd Bonner

Byrd Bonner

Theatre artist & critic
2025 ATAC Jasmina Wellinghoff
Award for Special Contribution
to San Antonio Theatre

"Director Blake Hamman and his creative team do not intend it to be for the faint of heart. Sexuality, politics and terror are palpable and all but explicit here."

Bonner — who has seen upwards of a dozen productions of Cabaret across his career — praised the production for the clarity and ferocity of its political vision. He described Katy Sisco's "What Would You Do?" as the climax of the production's arc and "the highlight of the show," and singled out Jackson Gable's Emcee as embodying the required combination of repulsion and seduction.

The staging of "Money" was called "the best in memory, superb and hitting every mark." Alonzo Corona's choreography was described as "gymnastically degenerate and delightful — but joyless. As it should be."

Bonner praised Blake's use of the ensemble, noting that "every chorus boy and girl, as well as every randy sailor, have clearly defined characters." He highlighted Blake's incorporation of anthropomorphic set elements and his handling of fight choreography by Morgan Clyde as "gasp-inducing."

On the production's ambitions, Bonner wrote that the show "succeeds where some versions of Cabaret do not: it puts us on notice and condemns political apathy" — placing the production in direct lineage with Hal Prince's original 1966 Broadway vision.

"In Hamman's director's notes he refers to the mirror of Cabaret… As I left the Wonder I was thinking not of Weimar Germany, but of the air I was breathing."

The Story

Berlin, 1930. Welcome to the Cabaret.

Weimar Germany. The Kit Kat Klub glitters with music, spectacle, and willful forgetting. American writer Cliff Bradshaw arrives in Berlin and falls into its orbit — the nightclub, the boarding house, the performer Sally Bowles, who lives entirely in the present tense. The Emcee watches it all, narrating the descent.

As the political temperature rises outside, the Klub's lights burn brighter. Fräulein Schneider and Herr Schultz find love against an increasingly hostile backdrop. Sally chooses the show. Cliff chooses to leave. The Emcee remains.

The landmark 1966 musical — with its songs, its mirror, and its refusal to comfort — has never been more urgent. This production does not look away.

Also Reviewed

Tim Hedgepeth

Theatre artist & peer
San Antonio theatre community
Facebook · Opening Night · May 2026

"Earlier this season, the Wonder Theatre presented a smashing revival of Sweeney Todd… Last night, again at the Wonder, I experienced the same kind of thrill when Blake Hamman's company premiered a pretty damn glorious take on Kander & Ebb's Cabaret."

Hedgepeth praised the production for bringing "raunch and rage plus a generous splattering of Brecht to make even the most jaded playgoer take notice." He highlighted Grace Lynn and Jackson Gable as embodying their characters with "all the confidence, grit, and raw nerve required," and specifically praised Blake's "surprising character twists for the Emcee."

He described Kayla Hernandez Friend's Fräulein Kost as earning "some extra stage time" to notable effect, and praised the onstage orchestra as allowing the audience to "hear the intricacies of the original score" from seven musicians. He called Alonzo Corona's choreography "the best… to date — gymnastically degenerate and delightful."

Hedgepeth closed by crediting Wonder Theatre artistic director Chris Rodriguez for "bold risk-taking" in programming both this production and Sweeney Todd, and situating the production alongside Hal Prince's original vision: a Cabaret that "puts us on notice and condemns political apathy."

Director's Statement

On Directing
Cabaret

"Cabaret is a mirror. The question is whether the audience is willing to look into it."

Every production of Cabaret is a political act. The show was written as one, premiered as one, and has been revived as one ever since. The Emcee is not a host — he is a diagnosis. The Kit Kat Klub is not an escape — it is the place we go to avoid what's coming.

This production was made with a full onstage orchestra, a cast committed to the full weight of the material, and a set — designed by Benjamin Grabill — that puts the audience inside the maze of the boarding house and the Klub simultaneously. The goal was to remove the distance between the audience and the story. To make it impossible to watch from safety.

Christopher Isherwood wrote: "I am a camera with its shutter open, quite passive, recording, not thinking." This production asked: what happens when the camera turns around?

— Blake Hamman, Director
